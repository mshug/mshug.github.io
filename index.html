Course Description | What is GIS for Designers?

Maps are rhetorical tools. Designers craft compelling arguments with maps, mapping, and spatial data visualization. Maps represent vast, complex data; they can be seductive and powerfully dense. Designers use maps — not just of geography, but of social, domestic, and political context — to analyze and understand spatial problems, and argue for data-defensible solutions.

This website showcases the course materials and examples of student work from a class called GIS for Designers which was developed at Virginia Tech School of Architecture between 2022-2023. The course was both seminar and workshop, splitting its time between technical introductions to free, open-source tools like QGIS, Leaflet, and OpenStreetMap, and reading discussions which emphasized the non-neutrality of mapping practices over time, and the role of contemporary tools to rectify this with practices like community mapping. The full syllabus for two iterations of the class is linked below.

The class positioned itself specifically as a social mapping class: that is, students learned not only how to map, but the potential of mapping to reinforce or to disrupt narratives. Students looked at mappable issues within their own geography: the Appalachian region around Virginia Tech. The final assignment took the form of a storymap (or, scrollymap), an interactive storytelling format where students tackled spatial, data-based issues like the legacies of redlining in Atlanta's heat island effect, the inequality to broadband access perpetuated by internet companies in Appalachia, and food access in Lynchburg, VA. Some students went on the translate their final projects into static maps for Virginia Tech's annual OGIS conference, where several won prizes in the student poster competition.

Below, you will find the full course materials developed with the help of several generous grants and four classes of studnet feedback. The tutorial pdfs cover the basics of finding and evaluating data, representing data sets in GIS, using GIS tools to dig into the meaning of data, and making visual arguments in the form of data maps. They also show how to create online, interactive storymaps using Mapbox and Leaflet. Some of this content is also covered in the lecture videos, though they are primarily oriented toward the class discussions and readings on the history of mapping.
